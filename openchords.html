Reading Chord Diagrams

The 6 vertical lines on a chord diagram represent the 6 strings of the guitar. The line at the far right is the High E (highest pitch) and the far left is the Low E (Lowest pitch). The horizontal lines represent the different frets. The dots the grid show what string should be held down at what fret. Sometimes chord diagrams will have numbers on the dots indicating which finger should be used to play the note.

Tips for Beginers to Learn Chords

  • Play around with the different fingerings of chords. Just because the diagram says to play the chord one way does not always mean that it is the best way to play the chord for you. Along with that, one fingering may be good for one song, and another fingering may be good for another. It all depends on whats being played.
  • When first learning a chord, place your fingers into position and press down and strum. If the chord does not sound right, check all of the strings, one by one, until you find the problem, then adjust.
  • Be patient. Everything takes time.

Natural Progressions of Chords

*Some of these chords are not the open chords. Instead they are barre chords*
Some chords naturally sound good together while others don't. These are called natural progressions.
These are the chords in their natural progressions

  • A,D,E
  • B,E,F
  • C,F,G
  • D,G,A
  • E,A,B
  • F,B,C
  • G,C,D
