Barre Chords

What is a Barre Chord?

A barre chord (also know as bar chords) are when you use your index finger to hold down all six strings of the guitar so that ou can use your remaining fingers to play the rest of the chord. Any chord can be played as a barre chord, because the barre chord is named by its root note (the lowest note). Barre chords are useful because they can be used all the way down the neck.

Finding the Root Note

To name a chord, we must find the root note. The root note is the lowest pitched note being played. Barre chords can be played with the root note on either the Low E or the A strings, so to find the root note of a chord, you must find the note being played on the lowest string being played.

Making the Chord

Low E String

To make the chord, you would "barre" (hold down all strings of a fret with index finger) a fret, and use your other three fingers to make the shape of either the E chord for major chords or the Em chord for minor chords.

A String

To make the chord on the A string, you would barre the fret, and use the other three fingers to make the shape of either the A for major chords or the Am for minor chords.

Missalanious Tips

  • When barring a fret, use the bony edge of the finger instead of the soft flesh on the anterior part of the finger.
  • The hardest part about playing barre chords is gaining the finger strength necessary to barre the frets. The only good way to get this finger strenght is to keep on forcing yourself to play the chords.
